Lesson 0003 · D&D 5e

Combat & Action Economy

Everything that happens in a single fight — and what your DM bot needs to track to adjudicate it.

You've felt this in BG3 without naming it. A rogue gets one Attack action but uses a Bonus Action to hide. A paladin casts Smite on a hit but can't use that reaction on their own turn. These aren't bugs — they're the action economy, the resource system underneath every combat turn. Understanding it is what separates a DM who rules correctly from one who wings it.

D&D 5e The Combat Loop

Combat follows a strict sequence.[SRD 5.2] The bot must enforce it:

1
Surprise check — if any combatants are unaware, they are surprised and skip their first turn entirely. The DM decides who is surprised.
2
Roll Initiative — every combatant rolls d20 + DEX modifier. This sets the turn order for the entire encounter. Ties: players choose among themselves; DM chooses among monsters.
3
Take turns — highest initiative goes first. Each combatant takes a full turn (see below), then the next, cycling through the order until combat ends.
4
Start new round — when everyone has gone once, a new round begins. Most effects that "last until the start of your next turn" tick here. Repeat until one side is dead, fled, or surrendered.
Initiative = d20 + DEX modifier

D&D 5e Your Turn: The Four Resources

Each turn, a creature gets four distinct resources. They do not stack, they do not carry over, and they are not interchangeable:

Movement
Speed in feet. Can be split before/after action. Default 30 ft.
Action
The main event. Attack, cast a spell, Help, Dodge, Dash, etc.
Bonus Action
Only usable if a rule specifically grants one. Not a free mini-action.
Reaction
Once per round, on anyone's turn (not just yours). Opportunity attacks, Shield spell, etc.
The biggest DM mistake: treating Bonus Actions as "free." They are not. A Bonus Action only exists when a feature, spell, or rule explicitly says "as a bonus action." If nothing grants you one, you don't have one. This trips up new DMs constantly.

Common Action choices

ActionWhat it does

D&D 5e The Attack Roll

When a creature takes the Attack action, each individual attack resolves in three steps:

1
Roll to hit
d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus (if proficient) vs. target's AC
Ability modifier: STR for melee, DEX for ranged (or finesse weapons). Proficiency bonus applies if you're proficient with the weapon. Meet or beat the AC to hit.
2
Roll damage — if the attack hit, roll the weapon's damage die and add the same ability modifier used to hit.
damage die + ability modifier
Example: longsword (1d8) + STR +3 → roll 1d8, add 3.
3
Apply damage — subtract from the target's current HP. At 0 HP, the creature falls unconscious (if a PC) or dies (if a monster, unless the DM decides otherwise).

Critical Hit

A natural 20 (the die shows 20, before modifiers) is a critical hit: roll all damage dice twice and add them together, then add the modifier once. A natural 1 always misses regardless of modifiers.

Crit damage = (damage die × 2) + ability modifier
e.g. longsword crit = roll 2d8 + STR modifier

Armour Class (AC)

AC is a static defence value — the number the attacker must meet or beat. It comes from armor, shield, and in some cases DEX modifier:

Armor typeBase AC formula
Unarmored10 + DEX modifier
Light armorarmor value + DEX modifier
Medium armorarmor value + DEX modifier (max +2)
Heavy armorarmor value (DEX ignored)
+Shield+2 to any of the above

D&D 5e Advantage & Disadvantage

This mechanic replaces a dozen situational modifiers with one binary state. You either have it or you don't — and they cancel each other out regardless of how many sources you have.

Advantage
Roll the d20 twice, use the higher result. Granted by flanking, the Help action, certain spells, attacking while hidden, and more.
Disadvantage
Roll the d20 twice, use the lower result. Caused by being Prone for ranged attacks, being Blinded, Poisoned, attacking while restrained, and more.
They cancel out perfectly. One source of Advantage + one source of Disadvantage = normal roll. It doesn't matter if you have three sources of Advantage — one Disadvantage cancels all of them. This simplicity is intentional.

D&D 5e Hit Points & Death

HP is the buffer between a creature and death. It has a maximum (set by class and CON modifier) and a current value that changes as damage is taken and healing is received.

At 0 HP:

  • Monsters — die (or are incapacitated if the DM chooses to knock them out)
  • Player characters — fall unconscious and begin making Death Saving Throws on each of their turns: roll d20, no modifiers. 10+ = success, 9 or below = failure. Three successes = stabilized. Three failures = dead. A natural 20 = regain 1 HP and wake up.
What the bot must track per combatant:
current HP  ·  max HP  ·  AC  ·  initiative score  ·  ability scores (for modifiers)  ·  proficiency bonus  ·  available actions this turn  ·  conditions (prone, unconscious, etc.)

Check Your Understanding

No scrolling. The friction builds the memory.

1. A rogue wants to use a Bonus Action to Dash on their turn, but no class feature specifically grants this. What happens?
2. A fighter (STR 16, proficiency +3) attacks with a longsword against a goblin with AC 13. The d20 shows 8. Does the attack hit?
3. A character has both Advantage (from the Help action) and Disadvantage (from being Poisoned) on the same attack roll. How many dice do they roll?
4. A PC drops to 0 HP. On their next turn they roll a Death Saving Throw and get a 6. How many failures do they now have?
